Electrical Unit Definitions

Charge (\(q\)):

Coulombs (C)

  • Charge is a fundamental base unit.
  • Charge can be thought of as the amount of water in a bucket.
  • The charge associated with a single electron can be expressed as follows:
\[ q = 1.6022 \times 10^{-19}C \]

Current (\(i\)):

Amps (A)

  • Current is the rate of flow of charge.
  • Delta charge over delta time.
  • Amps are measured in Coulombs per second, so one Amp is one Coulomb of charge per one second of time.
  • Current can be thought of as the rate of water flow.
  • Current is expressed as:
\[ i= \frac{dq}{dt} = 1A = 1 \frac{C}{s} \]

Voltage (\(v\)):

Volts (V)

  • Voltage is the amount of energy in an amount of charge.
  • Delta energy over delta charge.
  • Volts are measured in Joules per Coulomb, so one Volt is one Joule of energy per Coulomb of charge.
  • Voltage can be thought of as the height of the bucket over a pool (potential energy.)
  • Voltage is expressed as:
\[ v = \frac{dw}{dq} = 1V = 1\frac{J}{C} \]

Power (\(p\)):

Watts (W)

  • Power is the rate of change of energy.
  • Delta energy over delta time.
  • Watts are measured in Joules per second, so one Watt is one Joule of energy per one second of time.
  • Power can be thought of as the rate that the bucket is filling from a given height.
  • Watts can be expressed as:
\[ p = vi = \frac{dw}{dq} \frac{dq}{dt} = \frac{dw}{dt} \]
